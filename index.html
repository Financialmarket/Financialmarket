Intro

Financial markets facilitate buying and selling of financial instruments, claims or services. It fulfills the credit needs of the individuals, firms and institutions. It handles with the financial assets of different types such as currency deposits, cheques, bonds etc. It is described as a transmission mechanism between investors and borrowers through which transfer of funds is facilitated. It’s composed of individual investors, financial institutions and other intermediaries who are linked by formal trading rules and communication network for trading the various financial assets and credit instruments.

Types of Financial Markets

  1. Money Market: it is a market for short-term funds normally up to one year. It refers to the institutional arrangement which deals with the short term borrowing and lending of funds.
  2. Capital Markets: it is a market for issuing and trading of long-term securities. The term to maturity should be longer than 3 years. The securities traded in capital market are informally classified into short-term, medium-term, and long-term securities depending on their term to maturity. It is market for long term borrowing and lending of funds.
  3. Foreign Exchange Market: It is a place where foreign moneys are bought and sold. This market deals with exchange of foriegn currency, notes, coins and bank deposits in foreign currency units and liquid claims like drafts, traveller's cheques, letters of credit, bills etc.

Functions of Financial Intermediaries

Functions of Financial Intermediaries are basically classified in three parts which are as:

  • Maturity transformation: Deals with the conversion of short-term liabilities to long term assets.
  • Risk transformation: Conversion of risky investments into relatively risk-free ones.
  • Convenience denomination: Way of making the unmatched match which is matching small deposits with large loans and large deposits with small loans.

Stocks

How does the money transfer take place?

It can take place through the intermediation of a bank or through the exchange of a financial instrument such as:

  • Stocks
  • Bonds

In the first case, their savings are deposited with a bank that lends them to a company, in the second case, financial instruments are directly purchased through an authorized operator.

Buying stocks is equivalent to buying a part of the capital of a company.

You become a member; with your own shares you are entitled to participate in the company's shareholders' meeting even if this does not usually interest small investors.

The main aspect is that by investing in shares you can receive a dividend if the company produces a profit and decides to distribute it to its shareholders.

In addition, you can benefit from or have a disadvantage from the increase or decrease in the price of the security.

What decides the value, that is the price of the share?

For listed companies the value is established by the market.

It depends on many factors:

  • the company's prospects of generating profits
  • company dividend

How do I evaluate an investment?

An equity investment is measured by its rate of return.

The return is made up of dividends and capital gains.

The return depends very much on the selling price and can also be negative and you suffer a loss.

Bonds

What are they?

The term fixed income brings together all investments in the global debt and credit markets. It is also an investment style traditionally associated with the ability to contain any capital losses.
The most common fixed income investment is the bond. These asset classes are classified as income in that they provide a stable and generally reliable revenue stream.
A bond works like a subscription, whereby you lend your money to an issuer for a specified period of time in exchange for paying interest over the course of your investment. If all goes well, your investment, or principal, will be repaid to you in full at a predetermined term, known as a "maturity".

Types of Bonds

There are many types of bonds. A first large classification distinguishes between:
  • ordinary bonds, also called plain vanilla;
  • structured bonds.
Ordinary bonds can in turn be divided into two categories:
  • fixed rate, which attribute to the investor pre-determined interest;
  • variable rate, whose interest is not predetermined, but variable in relation to market rates. The floating rate bonds, other things being equal, are safer, as they always offer returns in line with those of the market.
Structured bonds are more complex and less easily understood. In particular, their "structure" is based on the combination of two elements:
  • an ordinary bond component, which may or may not provide for the payment of periodic coupons and which ensures the repayment of the nominal value of the security;
  • a derivative contract, which makes the investor's remuneration dependent on the performance of one or more financial or real parameters, such as indices or combinations of stock market indices, shares, mutual funds, exchange rates or commodities.

Can they be purchased?

Bonds, like all securities, can be purchased on:
  • primary market, subscribing to them when they are first offered to the public;
  • secondary market, that is, by purchasing them "on the stock exchange", at a time after their issue, by those who have already purchased them.
The presence of a secondary market allows the investor, if necessary, to sell his securities before the expiry. In the absence of a secondary market, the investor would most likely have to wait for the security to expire in order to disinvest his position.

Before purchasing them, we must be sure that we understand their operation, structure, yield possibilities and risks.

Risk

Typical risks are:
  • Interest risk: the interest risk, which concerns the possibility that the price of the security will decrease as a result of changes in interest rates.
  • Credit risk: credit risk (or issuer risk), which is linked to the possibility that the issuer is defaulting, in whole or in part, in the payment of interest and / or principal.
In this respect, there are securities of different risk levels, since not all Issuers have the same reliability. For example, a state is generally more reliable than a private enterprise and, in fact, the failure of a state, although possible, is less likely than that of an enterprise.

THE SUBORDINATED OBLIGATIONS

And even for the same issuer, not all bonds have the same risk. In fact, subordinated bonds exist, for which the payment of the coupons and the repayment of the capital, in case of particular financial difficulties of the issuer, depend on the satisfaction of the other non-subordinated creditors.

There are rating agencies that measure the reliability of an issuer by assigning a rating that identifies the related credit risk. The rating is nothing more than a judgment on the issuer's ability to meet its commitments.
  • Liquidity risk: liquidity risk refers to the difficulty of quickly and economically selling one's bonds before maturity. In this regard, unlisted securities are certainly less liquid than listed ones;
  • Exchange rate risk: the exchange rate risk that occurs if you invest in securities denominated in currencies other than the domestic currency and is linked to the variability of the exchange ratio between the two currencies.

Rating

The rating is a judgment that is expressed by an external and independent entity, the rating agency, about the ability of a company to pay its debts or not. The ratings of the rating agencies are used to calculate the yield on the bonds and therefore the cost of debt for the issuers. In other words, the rating represents one of the factors that establish the cost of the loan and the consequent repayment conditions. The rating can help investors to monitor the value of their investment over time: the eventual downgrade of an issuer by a rating agency is, in fact, a clear warning signal to the markets and individual investors who, otherwise, wouldn't have the ability to detect the needs to restructure its activities.

Types of ratings

(credit, debt, country ceiling)

The credit rating is issued on a company's debt, but it is only a specific type of rating. There are international credit ratings that evaluate the risks, and therefore the expenses, of an investor who wants to transfer the securities expressed in the currency of another country into the currency of his country. Then there are the debt ratings of the nations. Since the states are the biggest debtors ever, the rating agencies evaluate and classify the securities of their debt based on the capacity that the same states demonstrate to face them. Another type of rating is the so-called "Country ceiling rating" which values the risks of an investment in a state, that can implement measures to block any capital outflows from its borders. With this type of rating, investors can also evaluate this type of risk.

The close link between these last three types of ratings appears evident, but there are also other types of ratings that rating agencies are capable of issuing.

Issue of the rating

First of all, the balance sheet is analysed in all its components and some parameters such as the profitability of the company, its ability to produce resources and income, the return of capital, cash flows, the relationship between equity and the debt and so on. The analysts of the rating agency must in fact compare the parameters of the company they are examining, with those of the other companies in the sector in which it operates and must also analyse the characteristics of that sector and the market trend. The company under examination must then provide all the information and documentation necessary to the rating agency itself by filling a questionnaire prepared by the rating agency on the basis of the first information obtained. Once the evaluation to be assigned has been established and the publication of the results has been decided, the rating is published on a press release on the agency's website and through the main financial media worldwide.

Calculation of the rating

The rating is assigned by marking the rating according to different scales of values that vary to the agency that issues the rating itself. The scale of the values that can be assigned is however an alphabetical scale. Another ranking typically used by rating agencies involves dividing the analysis over a short, medium or long-term temporal scale that can range from a period lasting a month to a period long year.
